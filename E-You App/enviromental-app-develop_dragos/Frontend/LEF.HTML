How to have an energy-efficient home?

7 September 2022

An energy-efficient home is one that uses as little energy as possible for lighting, heating and cooling. In recent years, building green homes has become an important concern for builders and developers from both environmental and economic perspectives.
All over the world, people have become increasingly interested in low energy housing, which is approaching zero energy consumption. There is already a European directive on this subject, which will be enforced on the last day of 2020.This directive concerns the construction of passive houses, which provide thermal comfort with low energy consumption.
Passive houses have the highest energy efficiency standard in the world and aim to keep total heat, hot water and electricity consumption below 42 kWh/m² per year. Of course, a passive house with this level of consumption per year is a tough target to reach, but that doesn't mean we can't make our homes more efficient gradually each year until we reach the target.
Energy efficiency is not just about insulating your home, but also about having a heating system that relies on renewable energy, and a ventilation system that extracts and filters stale air without losing heat in the process.

Energy-efficient homes have a low energy balance. A home's energy balance is the energy consumed to ensure that the home's inhabitants enjoy high thermal comfort and superior air quality. The geodesic dome is an example of a highly energy-efficient building, and this efficiency is largely due to the shape of the building.
Since 2013, owners have been obliged to have an energy certificate for the house at the time of sale, and without it, sale-purchase contracts could be cancelled. Energy certificates are those that classify the house in a certain consumption category - class A, B, C.

How to build an energy efficient house

It's much easier to build an energy-efficient house from scratch than to increase the energy efficiency of a house already built.
At the design stage you have full control over the construction and can choose an orientation that will increase the home's energy efficiency, as well as the materials, from the start. Here are the most important steps you need to take to build an energy efficient home:
  1. Choosing the location for building the house
    2. If you have an energy-efficient home in mind, the lot on which it's built and the topography are aspects that can influence long-term costs.

    A house in the mountains consumes more resources than one on the plains, for example. Wherever possible, choose a sunny location that allows you to build a house with the lowest possible energy consumption.

  2. Orientation of the house
    3. The orientation of the house is an important aspect that homebuyers have been guided by for decades. A north-facing house will be cooler and use more energy, while a south-facing house is more economical.

  3. Rooms for increased energy efficiency
    4. On the south and east sides, bedrooms should be placed, and rooms that don't need much heat should be placed on the north.

  4. Choice of building materials
    5. Among the most energy-efficient materials are recycled steel, insulated cement board, structural insulated panels, low-e glazing, vacuum insulation panels.

  5. Choosing thermal insulation and waterproofing
    6. Home insulation is essential when talking about an energy efficient home. When choosing materials for house insulation, criteria such as thermal conductivity (k or λ) and thermal resistance (R-value) are taken into account. Polystyrene and mineral wool are viable options, but you can also go for environmentally friendly options such as polyurethane foam from plant materials, straw bales, even sheep's wool.

  6. Designing and choosing the roof
    7. Cool roofs are the most widely used in building energy-efficient homes and reduce energy consumption by up to 10%.

  7. Designing eaves and shading elements
    8. Stretches and other adjacent elements should be designed so that energy losses are minimised.

  8. Window design, sizing and selection
    9. Windows are where the greatest heat loss occurs so it is preferable to choose 4-season or low-e windows and high quality joinery that does not allow heat loss.

  9. Choosing an efficient heating/cooling system
    10. Heat pumps are among the most economical home heating systems. If heat pump systems cannot be installed, central heating systems, especially condensing ones, or air conditioners in the higher energy classes, can be used.

  10. Using natural elements to increase the energy efficiency of your home
    11. Tree barriers, shading pergolas are natural and inexpensive solutions to increase the energy efficiency of a building.

  11. Humidity control and ventilation
    12. Ventilation systems that also have a humidity control function help to save the energy used to heat and cool the home, and therefore contribute greatly to increasing the energy efficiency of the home.

  12. Heat recovery ventilation
    13. The heat recovery ventilation system is one of the most economical. The principle of operation is very simple: warm air is extracted from the house and its heat is transferred to the cold air taken in from outside, heating it. The system can also be used in summer to cool the house.

  13. Using alternative energy sources
    14. A home powered by solar energy through photovoltaic panels is certainly one of the most economical in the long run, as is the use of electricity produced by wind power systems. Another option, which is also used in Romania, in Beiuș for example, is geothermal water heating.

Increasing the energy efficiency of a house


If the building is already constructed and the house cannot be designed from the outset to consume as little energy as possible, we can turn to subsequent solutions. Those interested can access funds through the Green House programme, which aims to make homes more efficient. Through this programme, funds can be accessed for insulating the home as well as installing systems that provide electricity from renewable sources.
We can reduce the energy consumption of an existing house or apartment by:
  1. Insulating inside walls - with polystyrene, mineral wool or environmentally friendly materials such as straw bales
  2. Insulating the outside of walls
  3. Changing the windows - because most heat is lost through windows, changing them can greatly improve thermal comfort in the home
  4. Insulating the roof, loft or terrace
  5. Basement insulation
  6. Making the heating/cooling system more efficient
  7. Energy efficiency - using energy saving light bulbs, energy savers, energy saving equipment, new energy efficient appliances
  8. Heating efficiency of domestic water heating or reducing energy consumption
  9. Using intelligent systems to monitor temperature, humidity and other comfort factors - thermostats, for example, are a great help in saving energy
Energy efficient buildings are the future for our homes and beyond. Investing in energy-saving solutions will also save money and take care of the environment.
